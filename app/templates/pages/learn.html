{% call section_block("what-is-a-truss", "Section 1", "What Is a Truss?", "A truss is a framework of straight members connected at joints so that loads move through simple axial force paths. In the idealized form used for analysis, members carry only tension or compression.") %}

Plane trusses appear wherever engineers want strength with efficient material use. The form is common because it turns complicated load transfer into a clean network of axial members.

Common applications

  • Bridge spans that need long, efficient load paths.
  • Roof systems that stay light while covering large areas.
  • Towers, masts, and industrial support frames.

Core assumptions

Pin joints connect members.

Loads act at the joints.

Members resist axial force only.

At each joint, you write equilibrium in the horizontal and vertical directions and solve the unknown member forces.") %}
{{ formula_block("ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0", "Every isolated joint must satisfy equilibrium in both directions.") }}

Step-by-step example

1. Solve the support reactions from the global loading.
2. Pick a joint with at most two unknown member forces.
3. Assume unknown member forces act away from the joint.
4. Write ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0, then solve the unknowns.
5. Move to the next solvable joint and repeat until all member forces are known.
{{ callout_box("insight", "Signs carry meaning.", "A positive result under your assumed convention means tension. A negative result means compression.") }} {{ callout_box("mistake", "Do not guess tension or compression first.", "Assume a direction consistently, solve the equations, and let the sign reveal the real force state.") }}
{% endcall %} {% call section_block("determinacy", "Section 3", "Determinate vs Indeterminate", "Before solving, engineers quickly check whether a truss has the right number of members and reactions for equilibrium-based analysis.") %} {{ formula_block("m + r = 2j", "m = members, r = reactions, j = joints") }}

Determinate

If m + r = 2j, the truss has just enough unknowns to solve from statics alone.

Indeterminate

If m + r > 2j, extra unknowns exist and equilibrium alone is not enough.

Unstable

If m + r < 2j, the structure lacks the restraint or connectivity needed to hold shape.

{{ callout_box("insight", "The count is a fast filter, not a full guarantee.", "A truss can satisfy the equation and still behave poorly if the geometry itself is unstable.") }} {% endcall %} {% call section_block("stiffness-method", "Section 4", "Why the Stiffness Method Is Used", "The method of joints builds intuition, but the stiffness method is what makes modern analysis software scalable, systematic, and dependable for larger trusses.") %}

Instead of solving one joint at a time, the stiffness method assembles the full structural system. Each member contributes stiffness to a global matrix, the solver computes nodal displacements first, and then member forces and reactions are recovered from that displacement field.

1. Assemble stiffness

Each member contributes directional resistance to the full structure.

2. Solve displacements

The unknown nodal movements are solved for the entire structure at once.

3. Recover forces

Member forces and support reactions follow directly from the displacement results.

Young's Modulus

E

Young's modulus describes how stiff the material itself is. Higher E usually means lower displacement under the same loading.

Cross-sectional Area

A

Area changes the axial stiffness of each member. Larger A means the member resists elongation or shortening more strongly.

{{ formula_block("Member axial stiffness ∝ E × A", "Geometry often governs force paths, while stiffness strongly governs deformation.") }}
